# Abstract

Improving maize productivity in intensive production regions requires not only identifying yield-related factors, but also quantifying how much yield and profit could improve relative to current farmer management. This study developed a field-level, data-driven framework to estimate maize management improvement potential in the North China Plain of China. We used 81 summer maize field observations from Hebei and Shandong Provinces collected during the 2025 growing season, including management records, field-status variables, weather variables, grain yield, and input-cost information. Four yield prediction algorithms were compared under a leakage-controlled design: ordinary least squares, Elastic Net, random forest, and XGBoost. The selected model was then used as a regional response function for constrained maximum-yield scenario optimization, in which controllable management variables were adjusted only within empirically supported regional ranges. Profit was evaluated after yield optimization by accounting for scenario-specific input-cost changes. XGBoost provided the best validation performance, with an R2 of 0.652 and an RMSE of 1168.06 kg/ha. Feature-importance results indicated that irrigation method, plant density, sowing date, and irrigation frequency were the dominant management-related predictors of yield variation. The optimized scenarios selected drip irrigation in both regions and involved earlier sowing, higher plant density, adjusted nutrient inputs, and stronger irrigation support. Median yield and profit increased by 27.38% and 50.15% in Hebei, and by 17.55% and 46.41% in Shandong, respectively. These results show that constrained machine-learning scenario analysis can translate field observations into region-specific estimates of agronomic and economic improvement potential for precision maize management.
